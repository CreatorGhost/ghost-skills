# Pedagogy, script structure, and channel strategy

## The script structure that lands (the "why" pattern)
Reverse-engineered from the top-performing intuition videos (Syed Institute "Where do Sin/Cos/Tan come
from" 2.4M, Eddie Woo, 3Blue1Brown) + Mayer / CBE multimedia-learning research:

1. **Hook = validated confusion**, not a claim. Open on the student's own frustration:
   *"You were told to memorize this — nobody told you why."* (Don't open with "this is important.")
2. **Anchor the new idea to a familiar one.** Syed's killer move: explain trig ratios via π
   ("a ratio that's the same for every circle"). We use: *"just like π is the same for every circle,
   this stays the same for every angle."*
3. **Let the viewer discover it, then name it.** Show the pattern emerging from concrete cases, *then*
   reveal "this is called the …". The learner reinvents it before the jargon lands.
4. **Concrete numbers before the abstraction.** Plug in real values (30°/45°/60°; sin A = 3/5) so it's
   believable and tangible, not symbol-pushing.
5. **Real-world payoff + a board-exam application.** The "why we use it": e.g. given sin A, find cos A
   in 3 lines. This is what converts "pretty" into "useful."
6. **Forward hook + soft subscribe** at the end (every winning video does this).

## Craft rules (the polish that separates it from AI slop)
- **Spoken word = shown word.** The on-screen label must match the narration at that instant (say
  "trust it" → show `trust it ✗`; say "the hypotenuse" → flash the hypotenuse). Mismatches read as careless.
- **Spoken chapter lead-ins, never silent cuts.** Each chapter opens with a narrated transition while
  its title card writes — not a silent slide flip.
- **Flowing morph transitions (3b1b's signature).** Don't fade-to-black between chapters; let the object
  *become* the next thing — the triangle's hypotenuse literally transforms into the unit-circle radius.
  This reinforces that the ladder, triangle, proof, and circle are all *the same thing* from different angles.
- **Highlight as you name it.** Sweep a light along a side (`ShowPassingFlash`) exactly as the narration
  says its name; reveal its label on the same beat.

## Research findings (so we don't re-derive them)
- **Mayer / CBE "Effective Educational Videos":** segment (chunk), signal (highlight the one thing to look
  at), weed (remove extraneous load), dual-code (narrate while showing). Mayer's raw "under ~6 min" figure
  is **overridden by the owner's format ruling: 6–15 min, length set by the topic, explain it PROPERLY**
  — apply Mayer via chunked chapters, spoken lead-ins, and pattern interrupts (chapter changes, zooms)
  instead of shrinking the lesson. Speak ~**150 wpm** with energy.
- **Voice (LOCKED by owner, 2026-07-03): Sarvam Bulbul v3, speaker `aditya`, IS the production voice** —

- **Topic/clarity > production gloss** (3b1b's own stated principle). Don't over-polish a weak topic.

## Channel strategy (decided, don't re-litigate)
- **CBSE-first, ICSE tagged-along, math-first.** CBSE/NCERT ≈ 10× the audience, is the national standard
  (used even by state-board & JEE/NEET-foundation students), and is the only route to a Hindi audience.
- **Concept gems are ~90% board-agnostic** — "why sin²+cos²=1" is identical across boards. So one video
  serves both: title it to the CBSE/NCERT chapter, list ICSE chapter refs in the description. Make a
  *separate* video only where syllabi genuinely diverge (rare in math).
- **The gap (our lane):** Hindi/marks lecture marathons are saturated (Dear Sir 17M, GREEN Board 9M); the
  **Hindi + animated visual-intuition + CBSE-syllabus** quadrant is wide open. Position each video as the
  *"finally understand WHY"* companion to the exam grind, not a replacement.
- **Monetization:** YouTube ad CPM in India is low; this works as brand/funnel (→ courses/coaching) or as
  a mission project — not ad revenue. Cost to produce is ~the Claude subscription + a few cents of TTS.
- **Effort reality:** a polished manim video is real work; cover concept gems where visuals are uniquely
  powerful (trig, calculus, conics, vectors, probability, transformations), not rote-algebra chapters.
